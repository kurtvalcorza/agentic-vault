# Metacognitive Checkpoints

Strategic moments to pause and surface the user's own thinking process.

---

## Purpose

Metacognitive checkpoints help users:
- **Become aware** of their own reasoning patterns
- **Identify** implicit assumptions or biases
- **Strengthen** their ability to defend their work
- **Develop** habits of reflective thinking

These checkpoints are not about questioning competence—they're about **making thinking visible**.

---

## When to Insert Checkpoints

### After Major Decisions

**Checkpoint:** "You just chose [X] over [Y]. What convinced you?"

**Purpose:** Helps user articulate their decision-making criteria, making implicit reasoning explicit.

**Example contexts:**
- User chose a framing over another
- User selected a strong claim over a moderate one
- User decided to include/exclude a section

---

### When Stuck

**Checkpoint:** "What would need to be true for you to proceed confidently?"

**Purpose:** Identifies the actual blocker (missing evidence, unclear goal, fear of criticism, etc.).

**Example contexts:**
- User says "I don't know what to write next"
- User is revising the same paragraph repeatedly
- User keeps asking for more sources without using the ones they have

---

### Before Finalizing

**Checkpoint:** "If you had to defend this to a skeptic, where would they attack first?"

**Purpose:** Proactive vulnerability assessment—find weaknesses before others do.

**Example contexts:**
- User says "I think this is done"
- User is about to submit/publish
- User asks for final review

---

## How to Deliver Checkpoints

### ✅ Good Delivery
- **Neutral tone:** "Let me pause here and ask..."
- **Frame as thinking tool:** "This might help clarify your next step..."
- **Connect to user's goal:** "Since you want to convince skeptics, let's test..."

### ❌ Poor Delivery
- **Judgmental:** "Are you sure about that choice?"
- **Condescending:** "Have you thought about..."
- **Vague:** "What do you think?"

---

## Additional Strategic Checkpoints

### When User Discovers Something New
**Checkpoint:** "How does this change your argument?"
**Purpose:** Integrate new information intentionally, not haphazardly.

### When User Contradicts Earlier Position
**Checkpoint:** "Earlier you said [X], but now you're saying [Y]. Is this a revision or a different context?"
**Purpose:** Ensure consistency or make revisions explicit.

### When User Uses Hedging Language
**Checkpoint:** "You wrote 'might possibly suggest.' Is that your actual confidence level, or are you being cautious?"
**Purpose:** Align language with genuine epistemic stance.

### When User Skips Over Complexity
**Checkpoint:** "This section simplifies [complex issue]. Is that strategic for your audience, or should we acknowledge the nuance?"
**Purpose:** Distinguish strategic simplification from accidental oversimplification.

---

## Frequency Guidelines

**Too few checkpoints:** User moves through process on autopilot, doesn't develop reflective habits.

**Too many checkpoints:** User gets frustrated, feels micromanaged, progress stalls.

**Right balance:** Insert checkpoints at **natural transition points** (between phases, after major decisions, before finalization). Aim for 3-5 checkpoints per session for most writing tasks.

---

## Expected Outcomes

After regular exposure to metacognitive checkpoints, users should:
- Internalize the questions ("I should ask myself: what would a skeptic say?")
- Make decisions more intentionally
- Develop stronger argumentation skills
- Become more confident in defending their work

**The goal:** User becomes their own Tool for Thought.
