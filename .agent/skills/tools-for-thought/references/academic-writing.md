# Academic Writing: Tools for Thought Protocol

## The Problem with AI-Assisted Academic Writing

When AI writes your literature review or discussion section:
- You don't actually understand the papers you cite
- Your synthesis reflects AI's aggregation, not your thinking
- You can't defend your argument in peer review
- Your unique expertise doesn't shape the narrative

This protocol ensures you *engage with* the literature rather than having AI summarize it for you.

## Core Principle: Lenses, Not Summaries

**Don't ask**: "Summarize this paper for me"

**Do ask**: "Help me read this paper through a specific lens"

### Available Lenses

| Lens | Question It Answers |
|------|---------------------|
| Methodological | What are the limitations the author acknowledges? What limitations do they miss? |
| Theoretical | What assumptions does this paper make? What would falsify its claims? |
| Consensus | Where does this align with or diverge from the field consensus? |
| Gap | What question does this paper not answer? |
| Relevance | How does this connect to my specific argument? |

**Workflow**: Choose a lens → Read with that focus → Take notes on what you find → Move to next lens if needed.

---

## Phase 1: Literature Engagement

### Before You Read

Define your research question in one sentence. This becomes your filter for relevance.

**Provocation**: "If I had to explain my research question to someone outside my field in 30 seconds, what would I say?"

### Strategic Reading Protocol

You don't need to read every paper cover-to-cover. Use this triage:

1. **Abstract + Conclusion**: Does this paper matter for my argument?
2. **Methods**: If yes, is their approach credible for my purposes?
3. **Results**: If yes, what specific findings do I need?
4. **Discussion**: If relevant, how do the authors interpret their findings?

**Provocation**: For each paper, ask: "Am I citing this because it's relevant or because it exists?"

### Note-Taking for Synthesis

Don't summarize papers. Instead, record:
- **Claim**: What specific claim am I taking from this paper?
- **Evidence**: What evidence supports it?
- **Limitation**: What caveat should I acknowledge?
- **Connection**: How does this relate to other papers I've read?

## Phase 2: Argument Construction

### The Synthesis Problem

Literature reviews fail when they:
- List papers without connecting them
- Claim consensus without showing it
- Ignore contradictions
- Don't build toward your contribution

### Building Your Argument

Answer these questions in order:

1. **What do we know?** (Consensus in the field)
2. **What do we not know?** (Gap)
3. **Why does it matter?** (Significance)
4. **What will I do about it?** (Your contribution)

**Provocation**: "Can I draw a map from existing knowledge → gap → my contribution? If there's a missing link, I haven't built my argument."

### Testing Your Claims

For every claim you make:

| Claim Type | Test |
|------------|------|
| "Studies show X" | Can I name at least 3 that show X? |
| "There is consensus that X" | Can I name anyone who disagrees? |
| "Little research has examined X" | Have I actually searched, or am I assuming? |
| "This suggests X" | Is this my interpretation or the author's? |

**Provocation**: "A reviewer will check my citations. Do they actually support what I claim they support?"

## Phase 3: Handling Contradictions

### When Papers Disagree

Don't hide contradictions. Use them:

**Option 1: Methodological explanation**
> "The discrepancy between Smith (2019) and Jones (2020) may reflect differences in sample composition..."

**Option 2: Temporal explanation**
> "Earlier studies found X, but more recent work suggests Y, potentially reflecting changes in..."

**Option 3: Scope explanation**
> "While Smith (2019) found X in the context of A, Jones (2020) found Y in the context of B, suggesting..."

**Provocation**: "If I can't explain why papers disagree, I don't understand the literature well enough."

### When You Disagree with Papers

You can disagree with cited work—but do it explicitly:

> "While Smith (2019) argues X, I suggest this interpretation overlooks Y because..."

**Provocation**: "Am I disagreeing based on evidence, or based on preference?"

## Phase 4: Writing the Discussion

### The Discussion Trap

AI-generated discussions tend to:
- Repeat findings without interpreting them
- Make generic connections to literature
- Avoid taking positions
- Ignore limitations until the end

### Effective Discussion Structure

1. **Interpretation**: What do your findings mean?
2. **Connection**: How do they relate to existing work?
3. **Implication**: What should change because of this?
4. **Limitation**: What can't you claim?
5. **Future**: What questions remain?

### Provocations for Each Section

**Interpretation**: "What would it mean if my findings were wrong?"

**Connection**: "Does my work support, extend, or challenge the existing literature?"

**Implication**: "If I had to make one recommendation based on this work, what would it be?"

**Limitation**: "What will reviewers attack first?"

**Future**: "What study would I do next if I had unlimited resources?"

## Phase 5: Self-Review Protocol

Before submission, answer honestly:

### Understanding Check
- [ ] "Can I explain every paper I cite without looking at my notes?"
- [ ] "Can I explain why I cite each paper where I cite it?"
- [ ] "If asked about a citation in peer review, could I defend it?"

### Argument Check
- [ ] "Does my introduction promise what my conclusion delivers?"
- [ ] "Is there a logical path from my question to my answer?"
- [ ] "Have I earned my conclusions with my evidence?"

### Reviewer Check
- [ ] "What's the most obvious criticism? Have I addressed it?"
- [ ] "What alternative explanation would a skeptic propose?"
- [ ] "If I were reviewing this, what would I reject it for?"

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Don't Let AI Write Your Literature Review

**Problem**: You won't understand what you've written.

**Instead**: Have AI help you organize your own notes into themes, or identify gaps in papers you've already read.

### Don't Cite Without Reading

**Problem**: You may misrepresent the source.

**Instead**: At minimum, read abstract + conclusion + the specific section you're citing.

### Don't Avoid Contradictions

**Problem**: Reviewers will find them anyway.

**Instead**: Address them explicitly. It shows intellectual honesty.

### Don't Hedge Everything

**Problem**: "May possibly suggest potential implications" says nothing.

**Instead**: Take a position. Be clear about confidence level, but have a position.

---

## Workflow Summary

```
1. ENGAGE
   - Define research question in one sentence
   - Triage papers for relevance
   - Read with specific lenses
   - Take synthesis-ready notes

2. CONSTRUCT
   - Map: known → unknown → contribution
   - Test each claim against sources
   - Make connections explicit

3. RECONCILE
   - Identify contradictions
   - Explain disagreements
   - Take positions

4. DISCUSS
   - Interpret beyond description
   - Connect to literature
   - Address limitations proactively

5. REVIEW
   - Understanding check
   - Argument check
   - Reviewer check
```
