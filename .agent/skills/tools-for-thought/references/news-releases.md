# News Releases: Tools for Thought Protocol

## The Problem with AI-Assisted Press Releases

Traditional AI assistance produces press releases that:
- Sound generic and corporate
- Bury the actual news
- Fabricate quotes that no executive would say
- Miss the rhetorical intent

This protocol ensures you *think through* your press release rather than delegating thinking to AI.

## Phase 1: Story Discovery

Before any writing, answer these questions:

### What Story Are You Telling?

Press releases fall into narrative archetypes:
- **Achievement**: "We did something impressive"
- **Change**: "Things are different now"  
- **Challenge**: "We're responding to difficulty"
- **Vision**: "Here's where we're going"

**Provocation**: Which archetype fits? If you can't choose, you haven't found your story yet.

### Who Are Your Audiences?

Different readers need different framings:

| Audience | They Want | They Fear |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Journalists | Novel angle, quotable lines | Being used for PR |
| Investors | Numbers, trajectory, risk | Being misled |
| Customers | What it means for them | Being ignored |
| Employees | Context, pride | Being surprised |

**Provocation**: Write down the one sentence each audience will take from this release. If they're incompatible, you have a framing problem.

### What's the One Sentence?

Every effective press release has one sentence that gets quoted. Write it before anything else.

**Test**: Would a journalist tweet this sentence? Would it make sense without context?

## Phase 2: Data and Tension

### Surface the Tensions

Most announcements contain tensions. Don't hide them—choose how to frame them.

**Examples:**
- Revenue up 12%, margins down 3% → Growth story or efficiency concern?
- New product launch + restructuring → Innovation or cost-cutting?
- Partnership announcement + delayed roadmap → Acceleration or distraction?

**Provocation**: What's the tension in your announcement? How do you want readers to interpret it?

### Test Your Framing

For every key claim, ask:
1. What does this emphasize?
2. What does this de-emphasize?
3. Is the de-emphasized thing something a skeptic will notice?

## Phase 3: Quote Construction

### The Quote Test

Real executives say things that sound like them. Fabricated quotes sound like marketing copy.

**Bad quote** (AI-generated):
> "We are thrilled to announce this groundbreaking partnership that will transform the industry and deliver unprecedented value to our stakeholders."

**Better question to ask yourself**:
> "What does [executive] actually believe about this? What would they say in a meeting?"

**Provocation**: Read your quote aloud in the executive's voice. Does it sound like them or like a press release?

### Quote Functions

Each quote should serve a specific function:
- **Vision**: Where are we going?
- **Commitment**: What are we promising?
- **Acknowledgment**: Who helped make this happen?
- **Context**: Why does this matter?

**Provocation**: What function does each quote serve? If you can't name it, cut the quote.

## Phase 4: Structure Check

### The Paragraph-by-Paragraph Audit

For each paragraph, answer:
1. What new information does this add?
2. Could a reader skip this and still understand?
3. Is this earning its space?

### Common Structural Problems

| Problem | Symptom | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Buried lead | News in paragraph 2+ | Move key fact to first sentence |
| Quote sandwich | Quote-paragraph-quote-paragraph | Group quotes, separate context |
| Boilerplate contradiction | "Leader in X" but release says otherwise | Align or cut boilerplate |
| Missing "so what" | Facts without implications | Add impact statement |

## Phase 5: Final Provocations

Before sending:

- [ ] "If a competitor read this, what would they say?"
- [ ] "If the news were bad, would I write it the same way?"
- [ ] "What question will the first journalist ask?"
- [ ] "Six months from now, will this still be accurate?"

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Don't Let AI Write the Draft

Instead of: "Write me a press release about X"

Try: "Help me identify the tensions in this announcement and how different audiences might interpret them."

### Don't Accept the First Framing

The first way you think about the story is rarely the best. Test at least three framings before committing.

### Don't Fabricate Specificity

If you don't have a number, don't invent one. "Significant growth" is better than a made-up percentage.

## Workflow Summary

```
1. DISCOVER
   - Identify story archetype
   - Map audience needs
   - Write the one quotable sentence

2. TENSION
   - Surface data conflicts
   - Test framing tradeoffs
   - Choose emphasis deliberately

3. QUOTE
   - Write in executive's actual voice
   - Assign function to each quote
   - Cut quotes without function

4. STRUCTURE
   - Audit each paragraph
   - Fix common problems
   - Verify lead isn't buried

5. PROVOKE
   - Final skeptic check
   - Competitor read
   - Time-travel test
```
